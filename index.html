2014 Pacific Northwest Wildfires

The fires of 2014 seemed to be a continuation of the 2012 and 2013 fire seasons. Firefighters had no real down time between the fall fires of 2013 and the unprecedented rash of fires on the Oregon coast in January, 2014. During these incidents, Oregon firefighters were engaged in full wildland fire suppression activities only four days into the new year.

When it did rain in February and March, the weather brought disaster to a neighborhood near Oso, Washington where emergency responders, usually called for fires, were on the ground to assist. Washington Department of Natural Resources incident management teams responded to coordinate rescue operations, along with many DNR and other agency employees, local firefighters, and volunteers.

On the East Side, the rains triggered a drastic increase in fine wildfire fuels with a flush of grass and wildflower growth that created an unbroken, highly flammable carpet beneath trees and brush.

Oregon’s largest fire on record exploded through the High Desert brush and dry grass on private and BLM-protected lands near Burns. The fire grew so large, mostly in one day, the perimeter exceeded the freeway distance from Yreka, California to Portland, Oregon.

On the same day in Eastern Washington, the state’s largest ever wildfire started. It burned more than 300 homes, devastating local communities and the natural environment which draws people to live and recreate in the area.

Rare wildfires burn in January

Four days into the New Year, the first official wildfire of 2014 ignited when conditions caused the escape of fires meant to reduce fuels by burning limbs and other logging slash. The first wildfire burned 549 acres near the Oregon coast, and cost almost $300,000 to control.

On January 22, the National Weather Service issued a Red Flag Warning for Southwest Oregon because of low relative humidity and east winds. A Red Flag warning alerts wildland fire managers to weather conditions that could result in fire starts and cause fires to burn hot, spread rapidly, or behave erratically.

Over the next two days, escaped slash burns throughout western Oregon burned over 1,000 acres. In all, 21 January wildfires burned more than 1,600 acres in Oregon. The 10-year average (ODF) for the month of January is 17 acres burned.

Pacific Northwest Wildfire Coordinating Group Prepares

The severity of the 2014 fire season in the region was predicted early, said Bobbie Scopa, Deputy Regional Fire Director for the Bureau of Land Management and US Forest Service in Oregon and Washington. "There was a dry fall and winter with little precipitation. It's not like when it's wet in the fall and the fuels are wet into the winter freeze. Fuels were dry all winter. We were getting large fires on the coast in January."

The decision was made early to ramp up preparedness levels of fire fighting resources as the fire season developed. The BLM, Forest Service, Oregon Department of Forestry and Washington Department of Natural Resources were all agreed. "Nationally, people were supportive of our plans...they saw the same things we did."

A key element of preparedness decisions was pre-positioning of resources as fire conditions worsened. "We had extra engines, dozers, hand crews, and initial attack crews from back east. We got extra helicopters to units with severity dollars. We had 14 or 16 single engine air tankers across the geographic area almost the entire summer when we normally have four. There were three water scooping air tankers, three or four air tankers at Moses Lake, two or three at Redmond, and two or three at Medford. These were supplemented by aircraft from Boise, Idaho and by the DC-10 jet air tanker until it was needed for fires in northern California."

National Fire Prevention Teams ordered.

To counteract the increased threat of human-caused wildfires during the approaching season, the PNWCG ordered a National Wildland Fire Prevention Team. The team’s assignment was to support the prevention efforts of field units and to build fire prevention capacity. Capacity building included increasing the number of prevention personnel in the Pacific Northwest qualified to serve on or lead fire prevention teams.

The first National Fire Prevention and Education Team arrived on June 16. The last team left on September 26.

Work began with an assessment of human causes of wildfires across the region. Ignitions were mapped. Field visits and telephone interviews documented the human-caused fire problems. The current fire prevention programs were described. Communication needs and networks were identified. Community concerns described.

A twitter account and website were developed to enhance communication among field units and to disseminate communication materials and practices. Prevention products were developed and distributed to the field. An outreach to partners in collaborative programs was implemented.

Prevention capacity was developed though involvement of personnel from the region on the prevention teams. A long-term plan was developed to train and staff several prevention teams in the region. The prevention teams were able to address the immediate threat of human-caused fires by reinforcing established fire prevention programs throughout the region. Capacity building efforts will leave the region better prepared to cope with emerging human-caused wildfire threats in future years.

A Wet Spring

Incident Management Teams assist at fatal landslide

Precipitation seldom fell throughout Oregon and Washington during the fall of 2013 and into December and January, 2014. However, rains returned in February and March, making up much of the deficit, particularly over the northern part of the region west of the Cascade Mountains.

While the rains provided welcome – if temporary – relief to the persistent statewide drought, they spelled disaster for a neighborhood near Oso, Washington.

Incident Management Teams best known for managing large wildfires are, in fact, all-risk teams. In addition to wildfires, they respond to emergencies from floods and hurricanes to earthquakes and terrorist attacks.

The Washington Department of Natural Resources sent a Type 3 incident management team the same day. Incident management teams provide leadership, logistics, planning, and operational support. They pay and account for costs, order resources, assist the media to safely cover the incident, and provide information to help affected people cope with the disaster.

Desert rains stimulate growth, later fueling fires

On rangelands and in dryer forests east of the Cascades, spring rains broke the drought long enough for grasses, wildflowers, and other low growing vegetation to put on significant growth. As weather dried out again the lush vegetation cured to a highly flammable, continuous carpet beneath the sagebrush and trees. When lightning ignited wildfires in July and hot, dry winds blew, fires spread quickly. The Buzzard Complex on rangelands 45 miles southeast of Burns, Oregon burned 395,747 acres, most of it in one intense day.

Wet Spring

 

Weather extremes that led to fires in January on the normally wet Oregon Coast and a deadly landslide triggered by intense rains characterize the 2014 fire season. Later, the unprecedented one-day run of the Carlton Complex fires that burned 400 square miles and more than 300 homes will be followed by intense rains that flood fire camp, wash away roads, and endanger firefighters.

If experiences with wildfires since the turn of the century suggest a “new normal”, perhaps we should expect more complex fires seasons rather than longer, dryer, hotter fire seasons. Perhaps the new normal is characterized more by weather patterns for which we are not prepared, including more extreme weather on both ends of the spectrum.

The Fire Season Starts Near Bend Oregon

On June 7, the Two Bulls Fire just west of Bend, Oregon set the stage for an intense 2014 fire season in Washington and Oregon.

The blaze began as two small human-caused fires in an area of dense ponderosa pine and brush. Pushed by winds, the two fires joined and raced six miles across more than 6,000 acres of forest land on the first day. Although wind speed was estimated at less than 20 miles per hour, its direction aligned perfectly with fuels, says Craig Lenz, fire staff officer for the Deschutes-Ochoco National Forest and BLM Prine-ville District.

During its run, the fire burned past suburbs of nearby Bend, and was stopped just short of the Skyliners Road neighborhood the se-cond day. Still, 250 to 350 homes were evacuated, and many more were on alert. "The wind was mostly from the north," says Letz. "If the wind had shifted more to the west, we would have had more of a problem."

For its behavior and size, the Two Bulls Fire was very early in the season, says Oregon Department of Forestry Central Oregon District Forester, George Ponte.. "Watching the ongoing drought situation in May, the district was probably on course to at least as difficult a season as 2013."

"The fire came before we officially entered fire season, and we didn't have the staffing we would have had later on in the season," Ponte notes.

The Awbrey Hall Fire burned in August and the Two Bulls Fire burned in early June. The fact that both fires burned with similar intensities and rates-of-spread demonstrates how serious fire conditions were at the beginning of the 2014 wildfire season. The Two Bulls Fire validated the Pacific Northwest Wildfire Coordinating Group’s early preparations.

Hot & dry – but not necessarily more wildfires

It was not long before a series of lightning storms provided ignitions beyond firefight-ers’ fears. Officials suspect arsonists caused the Two Bulls Fire. Human-caused fires continued to plague hard pressed fire-fighters. People also started the 36 Pit Fire, last of the large fires of the season. But the 2014 fire season was powered mostly by lightning.

Working well Together

Ponte and Letz agree the relationships between federal agencies and the state departments of forestry are outstanding. "Simulations, exercises, and after season reviews all help," Ponte says. "I would hold the model we have here in Central Oregon against any model in the U.S. We've fought a lot of fire together and learned a lot of lessons."

"We work a lot with ODF, the Bend Fire Department, the Deschutes County Sheriff, and the Bend Police Department ," says Letz. "Everybody was at the command post right away. It was just after a pre-season cooperator's meeting. Everybody knew each other and what their jobs were."

"When you roll up to a fire, everyone is really comfortable," Letz adds. "Folks were ready...it was key to us being successful."

The Roots of Fire-Adapted Communities

The Two Bulls fire reminded many Bend residents and firefighters of the 1990 Awbrey Hall Fire. Both fires burned west of town and threatened neighborhoods. Both made six-mile runs through brush and ponderosa pine. But the Awbrey Hall Fire burned 22 homes and the Two Bulls Fire burned none. Defensible space helped make the difference.

After the 1996 Skeleton Fire burned 17,000 acres in Bend and destroyed 19 homes, Bend’s Fire Marshal, Gary Marshall, developed FireFree. With financial help from Safeco Insurance, local businesses, fire agencies, and private citizens, FireFree launched a campaign to promote a simple 10-step program for fire hazard mitigation around homes.

This was an early, successful effort to en-gage neighborhoods in wildfire prepared-ness and mitigation. Local projects like FireFree in Bend ultimately demonstrated the potential for citizen-based community wildfire protection and blazed the path to national programs like FireWise and Fire Adapted Communities. www.firefree.org

The state of Oregon has an aggressive Firewise program helping communities and landowners minimize risks from wildfire. Although the Twin Bulls fire was stopped before nearby Firewise practices were test-ed, there were benefits from past work done, Ponte and Letz agree.

"On the very south end of the fire the Deschutes and Ochoco National Forests had done a lot of fuel reduction work in the past and the Skyliners Road neighborhood had instituted a tremendous amount of Firewise practices," Letz says. "We were feeling pretty comfortable about being able to catch the fire if it got that far."

Maybe this is the new normal

More than 317,000 acres have burned in 36 Central Oregon fires this year, the second hard year in a row. So, what lies ahead?

"We've had pretty extensive drought," says Ponte. "It will take a lot of wet stuff to change that."

"After this year and 2013, three bad years in a row would be pretty unusual, though it's not unheard of," he noted. "Maybe this is the new normal."
